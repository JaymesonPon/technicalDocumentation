Introduction

Below is a quick overview of how one may practice. I note, though, there are many ways and areas to practice. However, the art/discipline of piano is a field with ambiguous boundaries. I, myself, have wrestled with what expectations someone should have when learning to play piano. One teacher may include music theory as a necessary part of their student's curriculum, while another may insist upon ear-training. I, generally, try to incorporate as many aspects of music in teaching piano.

You should not consider this an exhaustive guide, but it will hopefully give you an idea of common "practices" for practice.

Areas of Focus

Below are my primary areas of music to develop:

  • Sight Reading
  • Technique
  • Theory
  • Improvisation/Composition
  • Performance

I will focus mainly on the first two areas. Though all areas should be developed, the first two areas benefit the most from the practice methods I describe in this article.

Practice Techniques

The practice techniques for sight-reading and technique differ greatly. (Technique encompasses scales, arpeggios, and general dexterity and strength development).

Sight Reading

Sight reading, like all these areas has a number of different skills that are being used at the same time.

  • Eye-training
  • Note recognition
  • Pattern recognition
These all need to be practiced slowly and deliberately. One of the most challenging aspects of improving sight-reading is simply having a sufficient amount of material at graduated levels. Sightreadingfactory.com is a useful resource, though it is a paid subscription.

Technique

Here are three main ways to practice technique, as well as any musical passage:

  • Metronome
  • Rhythms
  • Mental
These can be mixed and matched with each other.

Time Management

When practicing, time-management is very important. Using spreadsheets can be very helpful. Even using formulas can have its benefits. See example:

x = minutes practicing technique A

If you set this up in a spreadsheet software such as Excel or Google Sheets, you can easily track your practice. You could set up multiple variables/categories.

x = minutes practicing technique A
y = minutes practicing technique B
z = minutes practicing technique C

You can decide how much time to allocate to each and also the overall amount of time you want to practice each session.
After a few months of lessons I think most students should aim to practice 30 minutes a day for 5-6 days. Technique practice may only take 5-10 minutes, so a breakdown of the formula might be as follows:

x = minutes of practicing Hanon exercise 1 in A major
y = minutes of practicing major scale in A
z = minutes of practicing 2-octave arpeggio in A major
 With actual values:
x = 4
y = 3
z = 3
Total = 10 minutes

Finally, you can set up a weekly goal for minutes practiced on different areas (like the 5 mentioned in the Areas of Focus section). This way you can track the running total amount of time you have spent on a given area or technique. This helps you adjust your priorities as you progress through a practice timeframe. If you wanted to practice 30 minutes a day for 5 days, that would be 150 minutes weekly. If you wanted to spend 10% of the total time practicing scales, 10% on arpeggios, 10% on reading, and 70% on the remaining areas, you could set something up like this:

Given:
Total time = 150 minutes
Priority %:
Scales: 10%, Arpeggios: 10%, Sight-reading: 10%, Everything else: 70%

Formula:
(category priority %)*(Total time)
 If you wanted to monitor your progress:
(actual time spent)/[(category priority %)*(Total time)]

The most common thing I do is set up a table with a breakdown of the task in different keys, since being able to (for example) play a major scale in ALL keys is important. You can also set up a table broken into days of the week.

Schools of Thought

I'm aware of two main focuses on facility - i.e., being able to play notes.

  • Finger strength/independence
  • Periodic relaxation

    • Finger strength and independence are rather self-explanatory. These can be developed by exercise like those of Hanon. Scales and five-finger patterns inadvertantly develop these, as well.

    Periodic relaxation may be a bit more ambiguous. The idea is that pianists play better the more relaxed they are. Generally, this is true. You can play longer and more consistently if you reduce the energy and strain on your body. You also do a better job avoiding injury. The general idea is letting gravity and the weight of your arm and hand propel your motion into the key. Of course, you still have to use your fingers to direct this force (and add some of their own), but the amount of force needed to play a note is much less if you harness the weight of your arm. This isn't always possible, since you can't "drop" your arm over and over for every note that is played.(Just think of playing even a moderately quick succession of notes, and you'll see you can never drop your arm that frequently). However, if you do this periodically, it offers regular relaxation to your hands and fingers.

    Ultimately, I think it is best to develop both approaches. You will need both.
